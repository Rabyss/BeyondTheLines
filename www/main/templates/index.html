What does it do?

Beyond the lines lets you perform linguistic analysis on text, webpages and websites, and compare the results from different sources.

In the website mode, the analysis can be performed on pages talking about a specific topic.

For example, you may want to explore if political parties express negative or positive feelings towards certains themes, or if newspapers are objective, or show some opinion biais. Try it with UK political parties about immigration, Europe or family, and the same topics about UK newspapers: immigration, Europe, ecology or family.

Note: We currently support only webpages in English. Coming soon: French and Danish.

Written text can be broadly categorized into two types: facts and opinions. Opinions carry people's sentiments, appraisals and feelings toward the world. These can be moreover negative or positive.

Number of Words per Sentence

The number of words per sentence can provide a measure of the complexity of a text. For example, the masterpiece In Search of Lost Time by French writer Marcel Proust has an average of 38 words per sentence, while that's of a children can be lower than 10.

Mood

The mood of a text is computed from the use of modality verbs and adverbs. It can be indicative, imperative, conditional or subjunctive.

Modality

The modality of a sentence represents its degree of certainty. For example, the sentence "I wish it would stop raining" has a rather low modality, whereas "It will stop raining" has a high one. Accuracy is about 68% for Wikipedia texts.
